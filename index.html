yoko ono

Early life

  • 1933  Yoko Ono is born on February 18 in Tokyo, Japan, into a wealthy and prestigious family.
  • 1945  Survives the Tokyo firebombing during World War II. The family experiences hardship during the war.
  • 1952 Becomes the first woman to enroll in the philosophy program at Gakushuin University, Tokyo.
  • 1953 Moves to the United States and attends Sarah Lawrence College, where she is exposed to avant-garde art and music.

Carrer in Art and Music

  • 1955–1960s Becomes involved in the New York avant-garde scene, collaborating with composers like John Cage and artists like George Maciunas.
  • 1964  Publishes Grapefruit, an experimental book of conceptual art instructions.
  • 1966  Moves to London and exhibits Ceiling Painting (Yes Painting) at the Indica Gallery, where she meets John Lennon.
  • 1968  Releases Film No. 4 (Bottoms), a provocative experimental film featuring close-ups of human behinds.
  • 1971  Holds the exhibition This Is Not Here at the Everson Museum of Art, one of her first major solo retrospectives.
  • 1972  Releases Approximately Infinite Universe, a feminist rock album.

Later Years and Recognition

  • 1980  After Lennon’s assassination, Ono takes time to grieve while continuing her artistic and musical pursuits.
  • 1989  Releases Onobox, a retrospective box set of her music, helping reintroduce her work to a new audience.
  • 2001  The retrospective Yes Yoko Ono opens, celebrating her influence in conceptual art.
  • 2009  Receives the Golden Lion for Lifetime Achievement at the Venice Biennale.
  • 2015  The Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) in New York holds Yoko Ono: One Woman Show, 1960-1971, a major exhibition of her early work.
  • 2017  Receives credit as a co-writer of John Lennon’s Imagine, recognizing her influence on the iconic song.
  • 2019 The Peace is Power exhibition opens at the Leipzig Museum of Fine Arts.

Yoko Ono's life is a testament to resilience, boundless creativity, and an unyielding pursuit of peace. From the ashes of war-torn Tokyo to the heart of the avant-garde movement, she shattered conventions in art, music, and activism. Misunderstood yet unwavering, she transformed grief into beauty and silence into revolutionary expression. Through whispers of instruction, haunting melodies, and fearless love, she taught the world that art is not just seen or heard- it is felt.
